In our project, we designed a triggering mechanism based on cell receptor specific combination and introduce the circular template on the cell membrane by using specific targets in order to assemble hydrogel by rolling circular amplification hydrogel around the cell and capture the cell.
Aiming at generating hydrogel in a constant system from a single raw material and with a high degree of crosslinking, we utilized and developed the combination of rolling circular amplification (RCA) and multiple-primer chain amplification (MCA). They were carried out as follows: firstly, a circular template was produced and added with primer P1 corresponding to it. Phi29 DNA polymerase and dNTPs were used as raw materials for RCA, so as to obtain a long ssDNA chain with periodic circular template complementary sequence. Control the time of this stage (RCA), and then add primer P2 corresponding to the ssDNA long chain and another kind of primer P3 corresponding to the original cyclic template sequence into the system. In this stage, phi29DNA polymerase finished amplifying a cycle with all these three kinds of primers.
Although the primer sequences were different, periodically amplifying the sequence accesses that the roles of template chain and complementary chain were continuously exchanged in the amplification, so as to complete the generation of a large amount of ssDNA in a limited volume. These ssDNA strands, which is in a large amount, continue to fold and weave, forming nests of DNA monomers that pile up to assemble the hydrogel we need. This design allows the formation of hydrogel in an environment where the process is always mild and the activity of the enzyme can be maintained for a certain period of time, so the strength and cross-linking degree of the gel can be guaranteed in the cellular environment. Moreover, the raw material is simple and we can control the length of RCA time to manipulate the number of the periodic binds then control the crosslinking degree of the hydrogel.

In this method, the preparation of single stranded circular DNA template is the focus of the whole system process. We used a general circular DNA preparation method to synthesize chain shaped linear DNA from known circular DNA template sequences, and designed end of the linear DNA as complementary fragments of P3, which can be combined to form all complementary sequences of P3. In preparation, we added the designed linear long strand DNA and P3 to the system at the same time, theoretically forming a linear DNA that is complementary to P3 end to end in the system. At this time, linear DNA transfers into the circular DNA with a nick and part of it is basepaired. Next, T4 DNA ligase is added to the system to fill the nick, and Exo I/III exonuclease is added to remove P3 basepaired by the outer end of the circular template, so as to produce circular template.
It is worth mentioning that this method based on ssDNA coiling and folding rather than constructing by whole base complementary, which enables us to make the gelling system work better on the cell surface without gelling off or misassembling. In that case, we need specific bind between this system and our target cell so our next goal is to introduce the template on the cell surface. As for CTCs, we hope that capsulated cells can be used for living cells detection and research. Therefore, instead of the antigen and antibody molecular targets, we adopted the method of aptamer binding.

EpCAM (epithelial cell adhesion molecule) was first found in colon cancer as a single transmembrane protein, which is involved in the regulation of cell adhesion, proliferation and differentiation. EpCAM abnormally expressed in tumor cells and expressed 100 times higher than normal level in primary breast cancer tissues, 810 times higher than normal level in lymph node metastasis. EpCAM was selected as the target to combine the circular template with the cell membrane through aptamer binding. We screened an EpCAM adaptor named SYL3C from the paper database and verified its binding activity.

Our project aims at capturing CTCs with DNA hydrogel and it is in need of the spcical initiation mechanism. This case is districted with two elementsï¼šFirstly, an aptamer sequence which specifically recognizes receptors on the membrane of circulating tumor cells and we choose the EpCAM which is mentioned above as the molecular target. Secondly, special inhabitor design which enables hydrogel to be specifically triggered by receptors on the cell membrane and assemble around CTCs. We mainly designed two triggering processes for hydrogel self-assembling.

The first design is based on the conformational transformation with hairpin structure. In terms of molecule level, our design includes three parts: aptamer sequence, linker sequence and primer sequence. At the 5' end of the aptamer, we attached the FAM fluorescent radical group for labeling the aptamer binding to the cell surface, and at the 3' end of the appalatant sequence we sequentially attached the linker and primer sequences. The aptamer and primer are partially complementary to each other and that makes the primer sequences silent in the absence of cells. When the cell binds to the adaptor, it competitively opens the complementary pairing between the primer sequence and the adaptor, exposing the primer sequence to participate in the formation of hydrogel. In this case, we selected 9 base complementary pairs and 10 base complementary pairs through modeling, and we named them as aptamer 1 and aptamer 2 respectively.

The second design is linear ssDNA habitor shedding. Structurally, our design consists of three parts. The first part is the aptamer-linker sequence with a Linker sequence with a length of 23nt. The second part is the inhabitr used to mask the adaptor and linker. The third part is the stem-loop structure containing the primer, named H1. Among them, the inhabitor can mask the adaptor and linker sequence in the absence of cells. When the adaptor binds to the cell, the inhabitor will be competed off to expose linker. As for the H1, the 5 'end of the stem-loop sequence is the complementary Linker sequence. In order to make the Linker sequence in the first part open the stem-loop structure, we designed that the Gibbs free energy of the complementary pairing between Linker and stem-loop sequence is less than that of the stem-loop structure. The 3 'end of the stem-loop sequence is the primer for rolling circular amplification, and the 3' end of the Linker is the adaptor that connects the FAM fluorescent group, enabling us to detect whether the adaptor binds to the cell.

In the process of interaction with circulating tumor cells, the aptamer sequence in the first part specifically binds to circulating tumor cells, making the masking sequence fall off and the Linker sequence expose. The Linker is complementary to the entire segment of the hairpin structure, which opens the stem loop structure to expose the primer and binds to the circular template to initiate hydrogel formation.
